The Discovery

A shadow in
the light

On April 10, 2019, the Event Horizon Telescope Collaboration released the first direct visual evidence of a supermassive black hole — a gravitational monster at the center of galaxy Messier 87.

The image, constructed from data gathered by eight radio observatories across four continents, shows the bright ring of superheated plasma encircling the dark central void — the event horizon itself.

What appears as a simple orange ring represents the boundary beyond which not even light can escape — the most extreme environment in the observable universe.

The Physics

Where spacetime
breaks down

At the event horizon, the escape velocity equals the speed of light. Beyond it, the curvature of spacetime becomes so extreme that all paths — including those of photons — lead inward.

The bright crescent visible in the image is brighter on one side due to relativistic beaming: the plasma moving toward us is blue-shifted and appears more luminous.

The dark central region — the "shadow" — is approximately 2.5 times larger than the actual event horizon, magnified by the gravitational lensing of light around the singularity.

Timeline

A century
of pursuit

1915
General Relativity
Einstein publishes his field equations predicting the warping of spacetime by mass, laying the groundwork for black hole theory.
1916
Schwarzschild Radius
Karl Schwarzschild derives the first exact solution, describing the geometry of spacetime around a point mass — the birth of the event horizon concept.
1974
Hawking Radiation
Stephen Hawking theorises that black holes emit thermal radiation and slowly evaporate — uniting quantum mechanics with general relativity.
2017
EHT Observations
Eight observatories across Earth simultaneously observe M87* for ten days, collecting 5 petabytes of data stored on half-ton hard drive arrays.
2019
First Image Released
The Event Horizon Telescope Collaboration publishes the first resolved image of a black hole's shadow — confirming predictions made a century earlier.
2022
Sagittarius A*
The EHT captures an image of the black hole at the center of our own Milky Way — 4 million solar masses, 26,000 light-years from home.
